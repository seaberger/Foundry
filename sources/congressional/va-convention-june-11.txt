On the Power to Levy Direct Taxes
James Madison, Virginia Ratifying Convention
June 11, 1788

Source: Elliot's Debates, Vol. 3, pp. 246-261; Papers of James Madison, Vol. 11 (Founders Online: 01-11-02-0074)
Transcribed from Robertson's Debates and ConSource.org

Note: Madison's June 11 speech on direct taxation was extensive (approximately 4,000 words in Elliot's Debates) but was delivered in a characteristically low voice, making portions difficult for the recorder to capture. The full debate on direct taxation continued on June 12, and Madison's complete remarks from June 12 are preserved in va-convention-june-12.txt. What follows are the portions of the June 11 speech that were clearly transcribed.

---

Mr. Madison wished not to exclude any light that could be cast on the subject. He declared that he would be the last man that would object to the fullest investigation; but at the same time, he thought it would be more elucidated by a regular progressive discussion, than by that unconnected irregular method which they had hitherto pursued.

[Madison then delivered his major address on the necessity of direct taxation powers, the practicability of congressional taxation, and the safety of the power regarding public liberty. He addressed the security against state government absorption, compared representation and electoral districts, analyzed the Supremacy Clause, and discussed the relationship between state and general government powers. He examined the economic considerations regarding requisitions versus direct taxation, and international commerce and neutrality concerns.]

If the General Government has a power of taxation, it could select the most proper objects and distribute taxes in a manner that they should fall in a due degree on every member of the community, and it would be limited to fixing the proportion of each State while raising it in the most convenient and satisfactory manner to the public.

To avoid disappointments and embarrassments, the General Government would likely throw public burdens on those branches of revenue more in their power, continuously necessitating augmented imposts. Throwing disproportionate burdens on commerce would discourage it and create political evils, particularly disadvantaging Southern States which import and consume more due to fewer manufactures, and would therefore pay more imposts, with increased commerce burden further increasing this disproportion against them.

Mr. Madison insisted on going through the business regularly according to the resolution of the House.
