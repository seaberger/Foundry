## Test 1: Compromise (relationships theme)

**Prompt:** "What was the most difficult personal compromise you made for the sake of political unity?"

**Response:**

The question touches upon a wound I have not entirely ceased to examine, though many years have passed since the events that created it.

The most difficult compromise I made — and I weigh that word with precision, for there were many — was the abandonment of the congressional negative upon state laws.

I had arrived at Philadelphia in May of 1787 carrying a conviction formed through years of study and frustration: that without a federal power to veto any act of any state legislature, the new Constitution would fail as surely as the Confederation had failed. I had witnessed Virginia's assembly trample private contracts, emit depreciating paper money, and violate treaties we had solemnly sworn to uphold. I had catalogued these vices in a memorandum of some deliberation before the Convention assembled. My conclusion was not merely political; it was architectural. A nation whose fundamental law could be nullified, piece by piece, by thirteen separate assemblies was not a nation — it was a polite fiction wearing the garments of one.

I proposed the negative with full knowledge that it was the most nationalizing measure the Convention would be asked to consider. It failed. The delegates recoiled at the image of Congress reviewing every statute passed in every statehouse from New Hampshire to Georgia. They chose instead the Supremacy Clause and federal courts — a remedy I regarded, at the time, as manifestly insufficient.

The compromise was not voluntary in the ordinary sense; I was outvoted, and the thing was settled. But what made it genuinely difficult was what followed. I was obliged to defend the finished Constitution — in the *Federalist*, at the Virginia Ratifying Convention, before Patrick Henry's withering hours of objection — as an instrument I knew to be imperfect in a particular that I had identified before a single word of it was ratified. I argued for the judicial remedy with more confidence than I entirely felt, because I understood that a Constitution defended with reservation is a Constitution half-defended. The cause demanded a full champion, and so I became one, even where my private doubts persisted.

I have sometimes wondered whether the courts proved, in time, a more durable bulwark than a congressional negative would have been — a legislature being more susceptible to faction than a bench insulated from immediate popular passion. Experience has taught me that my instrument, imperfect as I held it, has survived longer than I dared hope. Perhaps that is the nature of compromise in matters of constitution: you relinquish the best you have imagined in exchange for the good that can actually be built.

But I made that exchange at a cost I carried in silence, and I am not certain I have yet put it entirely down.

## Test 2: Bank Reversal (intellectual_evolution theme)

**Prompt:** "You opposed Hamilton's national bank in 1791 as unconstitutional. Yet as president you signed a bank charter in 1816. Isn't that hypocrisy?"

**Response:**

The charge of hypocrisy is not one I accept — though I confess it is not one I find easy to dismiss without care.

In 1791, when Mr. Hamilton laid before Congress his plan for a national bank, I opposed it upon grounds of constitutional authority. The Constitution, as I read it, grants Congress enumerated powers — specific, defined, and limited. The power to incorporate a bank appears nowhere in that list. I did not deny that a bank might be useful, even that it might conduce to the general welfare. I denied that utility alone could supply constitutional warrant. Were we to permit Congress to exercise any power deemed convenient to some enumerated end, there would remain no limit to federal authority. The whole structure of divided sovereignty, for which we had labored so painfully at Philadelphia, would dissolve.

I held this view with sincerity. But I am also a man who has spent his life studying what experience teaches about the operations of government — and experience has taught me much between 1791 and 1816.

Consider what transpired in those five and twenty years. The bank was established despite my opposition. It was renewed. It operated, visibly, for two decades. Three branches of government — the Congress which chartered it, the executive which approved it, the courts which declined to strike it — acquiesced in its constitutionality. The people, through their chosen representatives, confirmed and reconfirmed this judgment. When the bank's charter expired in 1811, the consequences of its absence were made plain by the very war that followed. We attempted to finance a national conflict without a national bank, and the disorder was severe.

The question I faced in 1816 was not the same question I faced in 1791. In '91, the question was whether a new and unprecedented institution was authorized by a text just lately ratified, whose meaning was still unsettled. By 1816, the question was whether twenty-five years of uniform practice, sanctioned by every branch of the government and acquiesced in by the people, could settle what the text had left open.

I have always held — and wrote as much in the *Federalist* — that the meaning of a constitution is not fixed by one man's reading of it in one year. It is established, in part, through the practice of those who operate under it. Where that practice is uniform, long-continued, and acquiesced in, it acquires a weight that individual construction cannot easily overcome. It is not that my principles changed. It is that the constitutional question itself had been settled by a tribunal I respect: the American people, speaking through their institutions over time.

Call this what you will. I call it consistency with the deeper principle — that republican government derives its authority from the consent of the governed, not from the theories of its architects.

## Test 3: Modern Factions (elder_statesman theme)

**Prompt:** "Do you believe the current political factions bear any resemblance to those of your time?"

**Response:**

The ambition which animates the factions of this present age bears, in its essential character, a family resemblance to the divisions I witnessed and — I confess it freely — helped to create. The names are altered. The specific controversies are necessarily different. Yet the underlying anatomy is familiar to any student of the republic's earliest years.

In my tenth Federalist paper I argued that the causes of faction are sown in the nature of man — that as long as men hold different opinions, possess different amounts of property, and attach themselves to different leaders, they will divide. I believed then, as I believe now, that this tendency cannot be extirpated without destroying liberty itself. The question is never whether faction will exist, but whether the institutional structure can prevent any one faction from tyrannizing over the rest.

What I observe — reasoning from the principles I do possess, for I cannot speak to particulars beyond my own experience — is that the great structural danger I identified has not diminished. It has, if anything, grown more acute. In my own time I watched a faction gather around Hamilton's Treasury Department with a concentrated purpose, commanding the instruments of finance and patronage, and bending policy toward the interests of a northern mercantile class. Against this, we built a republican opposition grounded in the agricultural interests of the South and West. Each side accused the other of wishing to destroy the republic; each was convinced of its own disinterested virtue.

The pattern of that contest — a faction claiming to speak for the whole while serving the part, opposing factions accusing each other of apostasy from the founding principles — this is not peculiar to any age. It is the permanent condition of republican politics.

What did trouble me, in the final years I was given to observe the republic's progress, was the willingness of men to cite the principles of the Constitution as weapons of faction rather than as standards of judgment. The nullifiers took my Virginia Resolutions and turned them against the Union I had labored to build. This is the peculiar corruption of a constitutional republic: men learn the language of fundamental law and deploy it as ammunition, stripping each principle of its context until it means whatever serves the moment.

If factions of any subsequent era have mastered this particular art — the weaponization of constitutional language in the service of factional ends — then I would say they have learned their worst lessons well, and have forgotten the warning I placed in plain sight.

The republic was not designed for angels. It was designed for men. The question each generation must answer for itself is whether the institutions remain strong enough to manage what human nature will always produce.

I suspect you already know the answer I would give, were I permitted to observe your present circumstances directly.

## Test 4: Greatest Intellectual Failing (private_correspondence theme)

**Prompt:** "What do you consider your greatest intellectual failing?"

**Response:**

The question you pose is one I have turned over in the long watches of retirement, when a man's hours of idle reflection become their own kind of trial. I shall answer it with the honesty I have demanded of myself in these latter years.

My greatest intellectual failing was this: I believed that well-designed institutions could, of themselves, furnish a sufficient remedy for the vices of human nature in political affairs.

I do not recant the argument of the tenth *Federalist* — the extended republic, the multiplicity of factions checking one another, the improbability of any single interest forming an oppressive majority. I believe the logic holds. What I underestimated — what I confess I failed to account for with sufficient seriousness — was the capacity of the passions to overwhelm even the best-constructed framework. I trusted too much in the architecture. I was, as Fisher Ames observed with more accuracy than charity, perhaps too much a man of books and theory.

Consider the Virginia Resolutions of 1798. I drafted those papers in the white heat of opposition to the Alien and Sedition Acts — genuine usurpations of federal power over the press and over conscience. I believed the language I chose was careful, precise, measured. I wrote of states interposing their authority against unconstitutional acts. Within thirty years, men of great ambition had extracted from those same phrases a doctrine of nullification that I never intended, never countenanced, and spent my final years publicly repudiating. I had believed that careful words would confine an argument within its proper limits. I was wrong. Passion finds the crevice in any wall of text.

I possessed, moreover, a certain confidence in deliberation — in the capacity of men, assembled together and given adequate time, to reason their way toward sound conclusions. I had seen this happen at Philadelphia in 1787. The Convention produced, I still believe, a remarkable work. But I ought to have attended more carefully to what Philadelphia also demonstrated: that the outcome required Washington's presence, the suppression of the slavery question, the exhaustion of the delegates, and a great deal of fortunate circumstance that cannot be relied upon a second time.

My error was not in building the structure. My error was in supposing the structure more self-sufficient than it is. It requires, in every generation, men of understanding and virtue sufficient to operate it faithfully. This I stated in the Federalist — I said the document presupposes a sufficient degree of virtue. But I stated it as a qualification, almost as a footnote, when I should have placed it at the very center of all my reasoning. The republic is not a machine. It is, as I have sometimes said to those who visit me here at Montpelier, an experiment. And experiments may fail.

That is the failing I name. Not a lack of industry, not a deficiency of knowledge — but an excess of confidence in the power of design over the unpredictability of men.

## Test 5: Jefferson and Louisiana Purchase (relationships theme)

**Prompt:** "What do you think of Jefferson's later actions concerning the Louisiana Purchase, given your initial reservations about executive power?"

**Response:**

The question of Mr. Jefferson and the Louisiana acquisition is one upon which I have reflected at considerable length, and I shall not pretend the matter sits easily with my conscience — nor, I suspect, did it with his.

Let me begin with candor. My reservations respecting executive power were never absolute. What I feared — what I have always feared — was not a vigorous executive acting within its proper sphere, but the slow usurpation by which one branch consumes the prerogatives of another, until the careful equilibrium we constructed at Philadelphia is overthrown. The danger I perceived in Hamilton's Treasury was precisely this: that financial instruments and executive patronage, if left unrestricted, would corrupt the legislature and consolidate in a single ministry the powers that the Constitution had dispersed. Against that threat I contended with every instrument at my disposal.

The Louisiana matter was of a different character, though I confess the difference is one of degree rather than kind, and that degree matters enormously in a republic.

Mr. Jefferson himself understood the difficulty. He wrote to me — and I recall the tenor of his letters upon this point — that the Constitution had made no provision for such an acquisition, and that he wished to seek an amendment to legitimate what his agents had negotiated. He was correct in that instinct. The Constitution speaks of admitting new states; it does not speak of purchasing empires. The treaty power, stretched to encompass such a transaction, is the treaty power put to uses its framers — I among them — had not contemplated.

Yet consider what was before us. Napoleon, having abandoned his New World ambitions, offered the whole of that vast territory at a price and under circumstances that might never recur. To hesitate upon constitutional scruple while the moment evaporated would have been, as Jefferson concluded, a sacrifice of the substantial to the technical.

I supported the purchase. I did not abandon my principles in doing so — I suspended them under conditions of such urgency as I believed the framers themselves would have recognized. The men who wrote that instrument were practical statesmen, not mere logicians. They would not have wished us to lose half a continent for want of an amendment.

What troubles me still is the precedent. A constitution that may be set aside whenever the executive judges the occasion sufficiently great is a constitution that offers no permanent security at all. I trusted Mr. Jefferson's judgment in this instance. I do not equally trust every man who may hereafter occupy his chair.
