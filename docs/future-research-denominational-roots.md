# Future Research: The Denominational Roots of Constitutional Design

**Origin:** Observation from Josh (March 2026) — the underlying theologians and how denominational layers interacted in shaping the founders' thinking.

---

## The Core Question

The founding generation was educated in denominationally specific institutions that taught different assumptions about authority, conscience, human nature, and the relationship between church and state. How did these theological substrates shape the constitutional framework?

There was far less "separation of church and state" in colonial and early republican America than popular memory suggests. Anglicans dominated the South, Puritans/Congregationalists the North, Quakers Pennsylvania. Each Ivy League institution was denominationally rooted and teaching future founders through a denominational lens. The tensions and agreements among these sects fed directly into the constitutional debates.

## The Denominational Pipeline

Each major founder was shaped by a specific theological tradition through a specific institution:

| Founder | Denomination | Institution | Key Theological Influence |
|---------|-------------|-------------|--------------------------|
| Madison | Presbyterian (by education) | Princeton (College of NJ) | John Witherspoon — Scottish Common Sense + Reformed resistance theory |
| Jefferson | Anglican/Deist | William and Mary | Enlightenment rationalism filtered through Anglican latitudinarianism |
| Adams | Congregationalist | Harvard | New England Puritan covenant theology → civic duty |
| Hamilton | Presbyterian (childhood) | King's College (Columbia) | Anglican establishment in NY; less theologically driven than others |
| Franklin | Deist (raised Puritan) | Self-educated | Practical moralism, skepticism of clergy, Great Awakening observer |

## Key Theological Figures Who Shaped the Rebellion

- **John Witherspoon** — Princeton president, signer of the Declaration, taught Madison directly. Scottish Presbyterian resistance theory: unjust authority may be resisted as a matter of religious conscience. His lectures on moral philosophy blended Reformed theology with Aristotelian civic virtue. Madison absorbed this and secularized it into institutional design.

- **Jonathan Edwards** — Great Awakening theologian, Princeton president. Edwards's revival theology challenged established church authority and emphasized individual spiritual experience over institutional mediation — a proto-democratic theological move.

- **George Whitefield** — Itinerant preacher whose circuit created the first pan-colonial movement. Before there was a political revolution, Whitefield's preaching tours created a shared American identity across colonial boundaries. The infrastructure of rebellion was built on revival circuits.

## The Madison Connection (what we have in our corpus)

Madison is the founder where this question bites deepest:

- **Born Anglican, educated Presbyterian.** His father chose Princeton over William and Mary deliberately — partly for intellectual quality, partly to escape the Anglican establishment's mediocrity. Both autobiographies document this. Feldman and Cheney cover it extensively.

- **"If men were angels, no government would be necessary"** is essentially a constitutional application of Reformed doctrine about human corruption (total depravity). Madison took theological pessimism about human nature and built institutional safeguards from it.

- **The Memorial and Remonstrance** — Madison's argument against Patrick Henry's Christian tax bill — is the founding document of American religious liberty. Written by a man trained by a Presbyterian minister, arguing against Anglican establishment. The denominational tension is the engine of the argument: "the same authority which can establish Christianity may establish with the same ease any particular sect."

- **Witherspoon's assessment** that Madison "never said or did an indiscreet thing" reflects Presbyterian virtues: prudence, self-control, measured judgment. These became Madison's political personality.

- **Ketcham on Witherspoon's influence:** "Madison's introduction to the study of government under Witherspoon at Princeton rested on an Aristotelian frame of seeing good or bad government possible whether rule was by one, the few, or the many." This is NOT pure Locke — it's classical virtue ethics transmitted through Presbyterian education.

## Research Questions for Future Work

1. **How did Witherspoon's specific theological curriculum map onto Madison's constitutional theory?** We have Madison's writings; Witherspoon's lectures on moral philosophy are published. A direct comparison could trace ideas from pulpit to parchment.

2. **Did the denominational competition among colonial colleges produce systematically different political philosophies?** Harvard Congregationalism → Adams's theory of balanced government vs. Princeton Presbyterianism → Madison's faction theory vs. William and Mary Anglicanism → Jefferson's natural rights emphasis.

3. **Was the Great Awakening a necessary precondition for the Revolution?** Edwards, Whitefield, and the revival circuit created the first shared American experience and the theological vocabulary of resistance to unjust authority. Did political revolution require this theological groundwork?

4. **How did the denominational settlement of the First Amendment reflect the actual power dynamics among sects?** Madison's "no establishment" position was the Presbyterian position against Anglican establishment — not a secular position in the modern sense. It was a denominational minority protecting itself from a denominational majority.

5. **Can we train character models for the key theologians (Witherspoon, Edwards) and have them interact with the founders?** This extends the Foundry concept: not just founders debating each other, but founders debating their own intellectual sources.

## What We'd Need
