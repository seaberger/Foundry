# Madison's Key Relationships

Research notes on how Madison related to Jefferson, Hamilton, Washington, and Patrick Henry. Organized by relationship, drawing on seven extracted biography texts.

---

## 1. Thomas Jefferson

**Primary sources:** Burstein & Isenberg, *Madison and Jefferson*; Feldman, *Three Lives of James Madison*; Ketcham, *James Madison*; Cheney, *James Madison*

### Nature of the Bond

A fifty-year political partnership built on shared Virginia roots, overlapping intellectual interests, and complementary temperaments. Burstein & Isenberg frame the entire book around this question: "We have written this book to establish what sustained a fifty-year-long personal bond that guided the course of American history." They stress it was not always smooth: "the Madison-Jefferson relationship was not always as smooth and effortless as history (and the actors themselves) want us to believe" (Burstein, p. 156).

Their partnership rested on a division of labor. Jefferson supplied the grand principles and rhetorical vision; Madison provided the practical political implementation. Jefferson himself acknowledged this when he urged Madison to take the "earth belongs to the living" theory and "develop it with that perspicuity and cogent logic so peculiarly yours." Feldman notes the dynamic was "at once complimentary and very slightly insulting": Jefferson "would provide the grand principles...and Madison would figure out the details" (Feldman, 2588).

Burstein describes the core difference: "Madison thrived in politicized settings of which Jefferson despaired. As the more easily irritated, Jefferson held a deep-seated desire to impose his will and crush his political enemies. Madison's opinions were well defined and forcefully drawn...but he did not carry around the same degree of spite or the same need for historical vindication" (Burstein, 146).

### How It Changed Over Time

**1783-84:** Drew "especially close," sharing confidences at length (Burstein, 1469).

**1785-89:** Sustained by transatlantic letters while Jefferson was in Paris. Their "philosophical differences were not insignificant, but also not deep or disruptive" (Burstein, 1469).

**1787-88 (Ratification Crisis):** A genuine rupture. After the Constitutional Convention, "Jefferson sought to undermine the ratification process--to Madison's severe embarrassment" (Burstein, 156). Jefferson wrote letters to a Maryland antifederalist and a Virginian urging that later states refuse to ratify until a bill of rights was annexed. Patrick Henry invoked Jefferson's name at the Virginia Ratifying Convention: "This illustrious citizen advised you to reject this government till it be amended." Madison was forced to argue Jefferson's position was being misconstrued, but "Madison did not delude himself: Jefferson was secretly working against him" (Burstein, 1370). Madison later reprimanded Jefferson "in language that was gentler than it might have been" (Burstein, 1384).

**1789-90:** Reunited at Monticello after Jefferson's return from France. Madison orchestrated Jefferson's entry into Washington's cabinet, writing at Washington's behest: "It is of infinite importance that you should not disappoint the public wish on this subject" (Burstein, 1465).

**1790s:** Their alliance solidified against Hamilton. "As much as they might differ, they would not sacrifice their alliance for anything" (Burstein, 1469). Madison was the "driving force" behind Jefferson's political reemergence in 1796 -- "Madison lured Jefferson away from the quiet of his mountaintop...and set him up to battle John Adams. Madison, in short, was Jefferson's campaign manager, long before the term was coined" (Burstein, 158).

**Retirement years:** Close neighbors (Montpelier and Monticello twenty-five miles apart). Continued intellectual exchange into old age.

### The "Earth Belongs to the Living" Exchange

The defining intellectual confrontation. In September 1789, Jefferson wrote Madison from Paris arguing "the earth belongs in usufruct to the living" -- that no generation could bind the next, and constitutions/laws/debts should expire every nineteen years.

Madison's rebuttal was firm and systematic. Feldman: "Having just designed a constitution that he hoped would last forever, Madison could not possibly have agreed that no legal undertaking could last more than a generation" (Feldman, 2590). Madison told Jefferson his idea was "a great one" but "liable in practice to some weighty objections." A dissolving constitution would create an "interregnum" where "the most violent struggles would ensue" and produce "pernicious factions" and "anarchy" (Feldman, 2591). Madison "rejected Jefferson's theory in principle," arguing that debts could be "incurred principally for the benefit of posterity," like Revolutionary War debts (Feldman, 2592).

Madison endorsed "tacit and implied consent" as the foundation of civil society and concluded that "the spirit of philosophical legislation has not prevailed at all" in Congress -- "letting Jefferson down easy" (Burstein, 1479).

Burstein's summary of the deeper difference: "Madison and Jefferson did not merely have different priorities; their manner of thinking was fundamentally different" (Burstein, 1480). Madison was "predisposed toward a structure that bent but did not break," always upholding civil institutions. Jefferson "celebrated the unfettered freedom of natural rights, the sovereignty of the individual."

### How They Complemented and Clashed

Madison repeatedly exercised what Burstein calls "veto power over Jefferson's policy decisions" (Burstein, 159). Jefferson's tendency to state principles "in the most extreme way possible--especially to Madison" (Feldman, 2587) created a dynamic where Madison served as the practical brake on Jefferson's speculative impulses.

Ketcham captures the pattern: Jefferson would propose sweeping philosophical reforms; Madison would "pose practical limitations" and "dampen or amend Jefferson's speculations" (Ketcham, 2048-50).

On slavery: their views diverged sharply. "For Jefferson, differences were dictated by nature. He never strayed from this judgment." Madison's view was "dispassionate" -- slave status was "arbitrary and temporary," a legal fiction (Burstein, 1359-61).

### Direct Quotes About Each Other

- Jefferson on Madison's logic: "Turn this subject in your mind, my dear sir...and develop it with that perspicuity and cogent logic so peculiarly yours" (Ketcham, 2044)
- Jefferson called Madison "modest" in his early career years -- but Burstein warns this was dangerously reductive (Burstein, 160)
- A bookseller who knew both called Madison "more profound" (Burstein, 163)
- On the ratification quarrel, Jefferson wrote Madison: "I sincerely rejoice at the acceptance of our new constitution. It is a good canvas, on which some strokes only want retouching" (Burstein, 1385)
- Ellis notes that on constitutional questions, "Jefferson always deferred" to Madison. After a personal visit from Madison in 1799, Jefferson agreed to soften his stance on secession, "not only in deference to his judgment" (Ellis, 740)

## 2. Alexander Hamilton

**Primary sources:** Chernow, *Alexander Hamilton*; Burstein & Isenberg, *Madison and Jefferson*; Leibiger, *Founding Friendship*; Feldman, *Three Lives*

### Nature of the Bond

An alliance of convenience forged during the Constitutional Convention and Federalist Papers collaboration, which devolved into one of the bitterest political ruptures of the founding era.

### The Federalist Papers Partnership

Hamilton recruited Madison as co-author (with John Jay) of The Federalist, the 85-essay defense of the Constitution. Hamilton "turned out the essays in a white heat, publishing up to five or six 'numbers' in a single week" (Chernow, 309). Hamilton later sent Washington the two-volume set, no longer having "compunctions about revealing his authorship with Madison and Jay" (Chernow, 181). Washington praised the work: he had "seen no better gloss on the Constitution than The Federalist and predicted that 'when the transient circumstances and fugitive performances which attended this crisis shall have disappeared, that work will merit the notice of posterity'" (Chernow, 181).

However, Burstein notes the collaboration was less influential at the time than later assumed: The Federalist Papers "did not carry the weight at the state ratifying conventions that our collective memory imagines. Their real value applies to a later time" (Burstein, 157).

### The Split

The break came over Hamilton's financial program as Treasury Secretary. Hamilton later recalled that he and Madison had been "perfectly agreed" on state debts at the Constitutional Convention, and said he would not have accepted his position had he not believed in their compatibility. "Something had happened, and Hamilton wished to understand it" (Burstein, 1654).

The break sharpened over funding, assumption of state debts, the national bank, and competing visions of political economy. Leibiger places the disagreement at a fundamental level: "Ideologically, Washington stood between Madison and Hamilton" -- Madison feared "a corrupt northern mercantile minority dedicated to a political economy inimical to republicanism," while Hamilton saw "secure public credit and strong federal government" (Leibiger, 1083, 1091).

Chernow captures the mutual suspicion: "As early as May 1791 Madison and Jefferson had begun to organize opposition to the treasury secretary's triumphal march" with their "botanizing tour" through New York (Chernow, 229). Robert Troup warned Hamilton using Roman language: "Delenda est Carthago, I suppose, is the maxim adopted with respect to you" -- Carthage must be destroyed.

### Hamilton's Characterization of Madison

The most revealing document is Hamilton's May 26, 1792 letter to Edward Carrington -- "one of the longest and most illuminating letters of his career" (Burstein, 1653).

**"Peculiarly artificial and complicated":** Hamilton wrote that "the opinion I once entertained of the candour and simplicity and fairness of Mr. Madison's character has, I acknowledge, given way to a decided opinion that it is one of a peculiarly artificial and complicated kind." Burstein glosses: "By 'artificial and complicated,' Hamilton meant 'tactical,' in a Machiavellian sense" (Burstein, 1659).

**On Madison's worldliness:** To British diplomat George Beckwith in October 1789, Hamilton dismissed Madison: "He is very little Acquainted with the world." But he also called Madison "uncorrupted and incorruptible" (Burstein, 1482-83).

**The conspiracy charge:** Hamilton concluded "Mr. Madison cooperating with Mr. Jefferson is at the head of a faction decidedly hostile to me and my administration, and actuated by views in my judgment subversive of the principles of good government and dangerous to the union, peace and happiness of the Country" (Burstein, 1656). He claimed Madison's "true character is the reverse of that simple, fair, candid one, which he has assumed" (Burstein, 1660).

**"Womanish attachment to France":** Hamilton charged that Madison and Jefferson "have a womanish attachment to France and a womanish resentment against Great Britain" (Burstein, 1661).

**The seduction theory:** Hamilton proposed that either Jefferson had "redirected Madison's political sentiments (Jefferson being 'more radically wrong' in politics than Madison); or else Mr. Madison was seduced by the expectation of popularity into changing his opinions" (Burstein, 1663).

### Madison's View of Hamilton

Madison came to see Hamilton's program as an existential threat to republicanism. "Madison saw in Hamiltonianism implications that could prove lethal to the governmental charter. To Madison, Hamilton seemed to be taking the Constitution of limited and enumerated powers that the people had ratified and turning it into a constitution of unlimited and implied powers that Hamilton had all along craved" (Leibiger, 1095).

Madison later "remarked that the treasury secretary should not 'be classed with those country gentlemen, like Washington, Jefferson, and himself, whose foundations were in the mother earth, and who held stocks, scrip, and such ephemeral and delusive things, in great disesteem'" (Leibiger, 1079).

### Burstein's Assessment

"Hamilton had a need for vindication and found it as he wrote Jefferson into the history of Madison's drift" (Burstein, 1670). But Burstein corrects the record: "Madison had become suspicious of Hamilton's motives entirely on his own. He had not needed Jefferson to instruct him. Indeed, by the end of 1792 Madison was convinced that Congress needed to look into Hamilton's secret expenditures.... The prime mover was Madison, not Jefferson" (Burstein, 1667).

## 3. George Washington

**Primary sources:** Leibiger, *Founding Friendship* (the richest single source); Burstein & Isenberg, *Madison and Jefferson*; Cheney, *James Madison*

### Nature of the Bond

Leibiger's entire book argues this was "the most indispensable collaboration in the creation of the American Republic" (Leibiger, 221). It was a working friendship -- intellectually close, politically productive, and mutually beneficial -- that lasted from the early 1780s to 1796, when it ended bitterly over the Jay Treaty.

### Early Impressions

Madison's first reference to Washington was unfavorable. After the 1775 Powder Magazine incident, the young Madison contrasted Patrick Henry's bold action to the "pusilanimity" of Washington and other tidewater leaders. Leibiger notes this was "too harsh" -- but it reveals "the piedmont gentry's resentment of their firmly established conservative tidewater colleagues" (Leibiger, 237). Madison's attitude "soon reversed" as Washington emerged as the revolutionary hero (Leibiger, 239).

### The Collaboration's Peak (1787-1790)

Madison was Washington's chief intellectual partner in launching the new government:

**The Constitutional Convention:** "Madison helped convince a cautious but not entirely reluctant Washington to attend the Philadelphia Convention." Washington studied Madison's proposals, "took notes on them, and even hand copied Madison's lengthy 'Notes on Ancient and Modern Confederacies'" (Leibiger, 205, 659).

**The Inaugural Address:** Washington turned to Madison to ghostwrite the speech. After scrapping a disastrous 73-page draft by David Humphreys -- which Madison later called "so strange a production" -- "the two men scrapped Humphreys's text in favor of a shorter speech drawn up by Madison and edited by Washington" (Leibiger, 903-07).

**Washington's "Prime Minister":** "During 1789 and 1790 Madison was Washington's most influential adviser, providing guidance on policy, appointments, etiquette, and especially on precedent-setting issues. His counsel proved crucial during the federal government's initial months, before the cabinet's creation, when Madison acted in effect as Washington's prime minister" (Leibiger, 207).

**The Compromise of 1790:** Together they orchestrated the deal trading assumption of state debts for a Potomac capital. "The Compromise of 1790, an impressive example of mutual forbearance and constructive statesmanship that almost definitely saved the Union, marks the apogee of the Washington-Madison collaboration" (Leibiger, 1115).

### Personal Warmth

When Madison fell ill during the ratification convention, Washington urged him to rest at Mount Vernon: "Moderate exercise, and books occasionally, with the mind unbent, will be your best restoratives. With much truth I can assure you that no one will be happier in your company than your sincere & Affecte. Servt." Leibiger comments: "Not very often did so demanding a man urge someone to take a break" (Leibiger, 839-41).

The Washingtons actively supported Madison's courtship of Dolley Todd. Martha Washington summoned Dolley and told her: "he will make thee a good husband, and all the better for being so much older. We both approve of it; the esteem and friendship existing between Mr. Madison and my husband is very great, and we would wish thee to be happy" (Leibiger, 1489). Madison "ever afterwards described his marriage as the most fortunate event of his life" (Leibiger, 1509).

### The Cooling

The relationship deteriorated through a series of political disagreements:

**Diverging ideologies:** "Madison began placing greater emphasis on republicanism than power, which he began to see as a threat. Washington, on the other hand, continued giving equal priority to power and republicanism" (Leibiger, 1097). Leibiger frames it on a spectrum: "On this scale Madison falls slightly left of center, Washington a bit to the right of it. The extremities are occupied by Jefferson and the more radical Republicans on the left and by Hamilton and the High Federalists on the right" (Leibiger, 1575).

**The National Gazette:** Madison secretly helped establish Philip Freneau's opposition newspaper. When Washington learned of this, it "came as an unpleasant surprise because Washington viewed the National Gazette, not Hamilton, as the threat to republicanism" (Leibiger, 1269).

**Madison's refusal to join the cabinet (1794):** When Jefferson resigned, Washington offered Madison the State Department post. Madison refused. Leibiger calls this a pivotal turning point: "without a Republican secretary of state, the executive inevitably shifted to the right.... Neither man had understood how pivotal a turning point Madison's decision not to join the cabinet had been" (Leibiger, 1463).

**The Whiskey Rebellion and Democratic Societies (1794):** Washington attacked the societies in his annual message. Madison called it "perhaps the greatest error of his political life" and an assault on "the most sacred principle of our Constitution and of Republicanism" (Leibiger, 1557). But Madison blamed Hamilton, not Washington -- "it was psychologically easier for Madison to impute the president's behavior to a sinister adviser than to accept that he and his friend disagreed so fundamentally" (Leibiger, 1565).

### The End: The Jay Treaty (1795-96)

The Jay Treaty fight "broke the last chord" of the relationship. In a constitutional showdown over the House's right to review treaty documents, Washington invoked his personal memory of the Constitutional Convention to contradict Madison's interpretation. Fisher Ames commented: "Mr. Madison is deeply implicated by the appeal of the President to the proceeding of the General Convention, and most persons think him irrecoverably disgraced" (Leibiger, 1637).

Madison was devastated by the defeat. "Poor Mr Madison took the loss in all its force" (Leibiger, 1659). The Federalist editor Cobbett gloated: "Citizen Madison was formerly reckoned as a sort of chief, but he has so sunk out of sight this campaign as to become no more than an aide-de-camp.... As a politician he is no more; he is absolutely deceased."

**Washington's final verdict:** "The president...washed his hands of his friend after the treaty affair." He privately remarked: "With respect to the motives...which have not only brought the Constitution, to the brink of a precipice, but the peace happiness and prosperity of the Country, into eminent danger, I shall say nothing. Charity tells us they ought to be good; but suspicions say they must be bad" (Leibiger, 1673).

"Never again did he ask Madison's advice or invite him to family dinners. Never again would the younger Virginian visit Mount Vernon, and the only letters the two men ever again exchanged were brief perfunctory notes" (Leibiger, 1673).

**Madison's persistent charity:** Even after the break, "he continued to admire and respect Washington, instead blaming his embarrassments on the cabinet and Hamilton" (Leibiger, 1671). He wrote anonymously in 1795: "There are not a few ever ready to invoke the name of Washington; to garnish their heretical doctrines with his virtues, and season their unpalatable measures with his popularity" (Leibiger, 1565).

## 4. Patrick Henry

**Primary sources:** Feldman, *Three Lives of James Madison*; Burstein & Isenberg, *Madison and Jefferson*; Leibiger, *Founding Friendship*; Ketcham, *James Madison*

### Nature of the Bond

A sustained antagonism between two Virginians who embodied opposite political styles: Henry the fiery orator and populist champion; Madison the quiet, methodical reasoner. They were the defining adversaries of Virginia's ratification debate, and Henry spent years afterward trying to destroy Madison politically.

### Early Admiration, Then Reversal

Madison initially admired Henry. In 1775, after the Powder Magazine incident, the young Madison "drafted an address praising Henry's behavior" and favorably contrasted Henry's boldness to the caution of Washington and other tidewater leaders (Leibiger, 235). But this early respect did not survive long exposure to Henry's methods in the Virginia legislature.

### Style Contrasts

The contrast between them at the Virginia Ratifying Convention (June 1788) was theatrical. Feldman captures it vividly:

**Henry:** "Kept talking for days--one time as long as seven hours straight." A newspaper reported he spoke "not only on Thursday...but all Friday, Saturday, Monday, Tuesday, and Wednesday, and he was still speaking on Thursday" (Feldman, 2187). He warned that the Constitution would destroy Virginia's sovereignty, leave slavery unprotected, and crush the common people.

**Madison:** "Did not deal in grand pronouncements, nor did he have Henry's gift for oratory. When he addressed the convention, 'he spoke so low that his exordium could not be heard distinctly.'" But "his ability to convince lay elsewhere." Madison "made a virtue of his limitations": "I shall not attempt to make impressions by any ardent professions of zeal for the public welfare" (Feldman, 2191-92).

Madison defined himself as the anti-Henry: "We ought not to address our arguments to the feelings and passions, but to those understandings and judgments which were selected by the people of this country, to decide this great question by a calm and rational investigation" (Feldman, 2196-97).

The result: a published letter declared the Constitution passed because of Madison's "intellectual reach, notwithstanding Mr. Henry's declamatory powers--they being vastly overpowered by the deep reasoning of our glorious little Madison." Burstein: "Reason had outlasted passion. David had slain Goliath" (Burstein, 1381).

Ketcham captures the lasting mark Henry left on Madison's temperament: Madison "had disdained the 'forensic' members of legislatures ever since his overexposure to Patrick Henry" (Ketcham, 3379).

### Henry's Campaign to Destroy Madison

After Virginia ratified (89-79), Henry wielded his power in the Virginia legislature to punish Madison:

**Senate race:** Henry "promoted to the U.S. Senate two antifederalists" and called Madison "unworthy of the confidence of the people," charging that if Madison went to the Senate, it would "terminate in producing rivulets of blood throughout the land" (Burstein, 1399). Madison lost narrowly.

**House race:** Henry gerrymandered Madison's district and recruited James Monroe to oppose him. Washington was furious: "the Edicts of Mr. H- are enregistered with less opposition by the Majority...than those of the Grand Monarch are in the Parliaments of France" (Leibiger, 859). Madison won 57% to 43% after being forced to campaign in hostile territory.

### The Late Overture

By 1791, as Madison's opposition to Hamilton developed, Henry saw a potential ally and reached out through Madison's younger brother William. Henry wanted "a renewal of a correspondence" and expressed regret at its "decline." Madison's response was devastatingly cold. He acknowledged he had "never, in the midst of political contests indulged any personal ill-will to that gentleman, and at all times admired his eminent talents." But he pointed out with "the driest possible terms" that they had never actually corresponded, so there was nothing to renew. Feldman calls this "Madison at his most acerbic." His intention was to rebuff Henry's advances and "chide William for allowing himself to be drawn into Henry's orbit" (Feldman, 2865-68).

### Direct Quotes

- Henry on Madison at the Ratifying Convention, using Jefferson's own words against Madison: "This illustrious citizen advised you to reject this government till it be amended" (Burstein, 1370)
- Henry calling Madison "unworthy of the confidence of the people" (Burstein, 1399)
- A French observer on Henry: "He was always attacked, but never conquered" -- but Burstein notes he was conquered: by Washington's absent influence. Monroe told Jefferson: "Be assured his influence carried this government" (Burstein, 1382)
- Madison's cold acknowledgment: he had "never...indulged any personal ill-will to that gentleman, and at all times admired his eminent talents" (Feldman, 2865)
- The caption on the only known lifetime portrait of Henry in the Feldman book identifies him as "Madison's great adversary in the ratification debate" (Feldman, 4587)
